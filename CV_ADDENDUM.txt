PROJECT 1 — Ride-Hail Trip Duration Prediction (Regression)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Built an end-to-end regression pipeline on hourly aggregated trip data
  structured like the NYC TLC Trip Record dataset (~14,400 rows, 30 days
  × 24 hours × 20 zones).
- Compared 7 algorithms (Linear, Ridge, Lasso, Decision Tree, Random
  Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost) on MAE, RMSE, R²; tuned XGBoost
  achieved test MAE ~13 s and R² ~0.96, beating a city-mean baseline by
  ~70%.
- Implemented log-target transform, multicollinearity pruning at |r|>0.85,
  5-fold cross-validation, and grid-searched hyperparameter tuning.
- Published as a runnable Jupyter/Colab notebook with full mathematical
  derivation of OLS, Ridge, and Lasso losses, including a manual β-estimate
  sanity check against scikit-learn.

PROJECT 2 — E-Bus TCO Parity Classifier (Classification)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Built a binary classifier that predicts whether an electric bus will
  beat diesel on Total Cost of Ownership over a 12-year Gross Cost
  Contract. TCO model parameterised from WRI India 2021 and ICCT 2024-25
  publications (capex, FAME-II subsidy, energy, maintenance, mid-life
  battery replacement, salvage value, 8% real discount rate).
- Compared 7 classifiers (Logistic, KNN, Decision Tree, Random Forest,
  SVM-RBF, Gradient Boosting, Naive Bayes); reported full metric suite —
  accuracy, precision, recall, specificity, F1, ROC-AUC — plus PR curves
  and confusion matrices.
- Decision-support framing: model outputs a probability ranked list for
  procurement officers, not a black-box decision; sensitivity analysis on
  diesel price growth, electricity tariff growth, and discount rate
  exposed in the dashboard.
- GitHub: <link>

PROJECT 3 — TAZ Clustering for the 4-Stage Transport Model (Unsupervised)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- Applied unsupervised clustering to socio-economic Traffic Analysis Zone
  data to discover zone segments for trip-generation coefficient
  calibration in classical 4-stage transport modelling (Ortúzar &
  Willumsen).
- Compared K-Means, Hierarchical (Ward), DBSCAN, and Gaussian Mixture;
  selected k via elbow-plus-silhouette; evaluated on Silhouette,
  Davies-Bouldin, and Calinski-Harabasz indices.
- Visualised in 2D via PCA; produced z-scored cluster profiles for
  planner interpretation; output integrates directly with the WRI India
  and ITDP Comprehensive Mobility Plan workflow.

================================================================================
INTERVIEW TALKING POINTS — what to say in screens
================================================================================

If asked "tell me about a project you built end-to-end":
  Open with Project 2 (the TCO classifier). Why? It demonstrates (a) you
  built a domain-grounded ground-truth label yourself from an industry
  methodology — most candidates use Kaggle labels; (b) you handled class
  imbalance and noise; (c) the business framing is concrete and
  defensible. Mention "I parameterised the TCO model from WRI India 2021
  and ICCT 2024 publications, computed labels for 60 routes, then
  compared seven classifiers."

If asked "what's the hardest ML problem you've worked on":
  Project 3 (clustering). The hard part isn't running K-Means — it's
  defending the chosen k to a planner who doesn't trust algorithms.
  Talk about the elbow plot, silhouette score, BIC for GMM, and the
  importance of cluster profiling for interpretability.

If asked "show me you can code":
  Open the regression notebook. Walk through the manual OLS β-derivation
  cell. This proves you understand what scikit-learn is doing under the
  hood — most data-scientist applicants can't.

If asked "what's your moat as a data scientist":
  "Domain depth in transport. A data scientist with no transport
  background can build a model on Uber data; they can't build the
  ground-truth label for an e-bus procurement decision, because that
  requires reading WRI and ICCT methodology and translating it into
  code. I bring both."
